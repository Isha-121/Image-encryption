Using DNA Cryptography

Rosalind Franklin examining a specimen under the mciroscope (left). Image from Wikimedia Commons, licensed under the Creative Commons Attribution-Share Alike 4.0 International license.

Rosalind Elsie Franklin, born in London 1920, was a crystallographer and chemist involved in several crystallography projects. After obtaining her PhD at Cambridgde University in 1945 and developing proficiency in carbon X-ray crystallography in Paris, Dr. Franklin joined the ranks of the King's College in London to study DNA. Even though not getting along with her new colleague Maurice Wilkins, progress was made and the famous Photograph 51 - capturing the helical structure of DNA - was taken by Dr. Franklin.

Both the image and other data of Franklin's research made its way to Watson and Crick, who used the data (jovially referred to by themselves as "burglary") to gain insight into and publish an article about the structure of DNA. Dr. Franklin left to join Birkbeck's college, where she excelled in studying the structure of the tobacco mosaic virus and was a recognized expert in her field.

At the age of just 38, Dr. Rosalind Elsie Franklin died of ovarian cancer. Several years later, Watson, Crick and Wilkins were awarded a Nobel prize for discovering the helical structure of DNA. Upon the rise of the feminist movement, this sparked the asking of the Rosalind Franklin question at every presentation of Watson, followed by applause. Although Dr. Franklin was not recognized with a Nobel prize, she is now remembered in a multitude of ways including, but not limited to, science institutes, awards, prizes and even a rocket planned to launch in 2022.
